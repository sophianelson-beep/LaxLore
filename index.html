Women's lacrosse originated from a much older game played by Indigenous peoples of North America, especially the Haudenosaunee (Iroquois). The original version, often called stickball, was played by men and had spiritual, cultural, and social importance.

Change Over Time → How Has It Evolved?

Key Moments (Catalysts for Change)

  • 1890s – Creation of Rules: Berenson established the first official rules, turning the game into an organized sport for women.
  • 1930s–1970s – International Expansion: Women's lacrosse spread to countries like England and Australia, forming international competition.
  • 1990s–Present – NCAA & Global Growth: The sport became part of the NCAA and expanded rapidly in schools and universities worldwide.

Over time, the sport evolved in speed, skill level, and equipment, while still maintaining its unique non-contact style compared to men's lacrosse.

Historical Thinking Skills

Cause & Effect

Cause: The adaptation of Indigenous lacrosse by Berenson into a school setting.
Effect: The creation of an organized women's sport that spread internationally and grew in popularity.

Comparison

Compared to men's lacrosse, women's lacrosse focuses more on skill and strategy with less physical contact. This difference reflects historical gender expectations in sports.

AP World Connection

This topic connects to cultural diffusion and globalization. A game created by Indigenous peoples was adapted in the United States and spread worldwide, showing how cultures influence each other over time.
